History & Location

The Konark Sun Temple stands on the coast near Konark, Odisha. Built in the 13th century by King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga dynasty, it was dedicated to Surya â€” the Sun God. The main tower (Shikhara) has collapsed over time, but the Jagamohana (audience hall) and the carved wheels remain as testimony to the builders' skill.

Architecture & Symbolism

The temple is designed as a massive chariot pulled by seven horses and mounted on 24 intricately carved wheels. Each wheel is about 12 feet in diameter and many have eight spokes, representing the time divisions known as praharas. The building shows deep astronomical alignment and symbolic sculpture (including scenes of life, deities, dancers, animals, and erotic sculptures similar in theme to Khajuraho).

Design features

  • Sundial wheels used for precise timekeeping
  • East-facing entrance to catch morning sun
  • Jagamohana (assembly hall) still standing
  • Precision carvings showing divine scenes

Materials Used

The temple was constructed using several stone types: chlorite, laterite, khondalite, and sandstone â€” each chosen for its carving properties and strength. Iron clamps and dowels were used historically to join stones. Some legends also describe a magnet in the top dome (now myth), which gave the structure the nickname "Black Pagoda" among sailors.

Sundial Wheels & Timekeeping

The 24 giant wheels are not merely decorative. Each wheel and its spokes were used as sundials: their shadows indicated time divisions during the day, demonstrating sophisticated timekeeping without modern instruments.

The wheels are placed along the plinth. Many have 8 spokes (praharas). Their precise carving allowed the builders to track time accurately â€” evidence of advanced astronomical understanding in the 13th century.
